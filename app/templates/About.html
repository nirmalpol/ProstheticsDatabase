    The traditional way of manufacturing of recreational terminal devices, which involves complex procedures like plaster casting, moulding, curing, remoulding, and finally fitting, is very time consuming, labour intensive, and expensive. Fortunately, 3D printing, a rapid prototyping technology that involves heating, exerting, and fusing the material layer-by-layer, has advanced to the point where it can create terminal devices for upper extremity prostheses for recreational activities (swim paddle, golf/hockey grips, weightlifting, etc.) relatively cheaply. It also has the benefit of faster manufacturing speed and less labour requirements.

    Nevertheless, there still is a significant cost associated with prosthetic recreational terminal devices that is not covered by provincial insurance (OHIP) and there is a steep learning curve to customize a design using CAD for the novice user. Customization (e.g., load-bearing, end-effector, size) may be especially important to make a 3D printed device that is safe for a range of users and particular activities. Furthermore, there are also concerns about the general safety and reliability of 3D printed devices in general as there are no regulations or standards that assess the structural integrity of 3D printed devices. This website is aimed at tackling this problem by providing free open source designs that have been vetted by our clinical team.

Purpose: To provide open-source 3D-printable recreational terminal devices to individuals with upper limb amputations that reduces the costs of obtaining a prosthetic while allowing size scaling for children and adults.
